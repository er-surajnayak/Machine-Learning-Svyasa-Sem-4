Principal
Component Analysis

When data has too many features, PCA reduces them by finding the directions where data varies the most — keeping maximum information in fewer dimensions.

01 · The problem

Why do we need data reduction?

Imagine predicting placement with 10 features: CGPA, study hours, attendance, internal marks, assignment score, mock tests, coding score, aptitude, communication, confidence. Now imagine 100 features. Then 1000.

More features ≠ always better. Too many features causes: slower training, overfitting, harder visualization, and many features may be redundant. Study hours and library visits might measure the same behavior — why keep both?
Problem 1

Slower training

More dimensions = exponentially more computation. 1000 features can make training impractically slow.

Problem 2

Overfitting

Too many features and the model memorizes noise instead of learning real patterns. Especially bad with small datasets.

Problem 3

Redundancy

Correlated features carry the same information. Math score and Physics score both measure "analytical ability" — one combined feature is enough.

02 · The solution

What PCA does — intuitively

PCA is a technique that reduces the number of features while keeping maximum information. It does this by creating new features that are combinations of the old ones.

Student skills analogy: Suppose you measure Math, Physics, Programming, and Logical reasoning separately. But they all reflect one thing: "Analytical Ability." PCA finds such hidden directions and combines correlated features into fewer powerful ones.
PCA in one line
Rotate → Project → Reduce
RotateFind axes aligned with max variance
ProjectMap data onto those new axes
ReduceKeep only the top components
Key insight: Variance = Information. More spread in a direction = more information in that direction. PCA keeps the directions with highest variance and discards the rest.
03 · Visual intuition

Seeing PCA geometrically

Imagine students plotted on a 2D graph: X-axis = Study hours, Y-axis = Internal marks. You notice points form a diagonal line — higher study hours correlates with higher internals.

Instead of keeping both axes, PCA rotates the axis to align with the direction where data spreads most. That diagonal becomes the new first principal component (PC1).

PC1 (amber) captures the most variance. PC2 (teal) is perpendicular — captures remaining variance.

Camera analogy: Taking a photo of a crowd from the wrong angle makes people overlap. Rotate the camera slightly and you capture maximum spread. That rotation is exactly what PCA does to your feature space.

Why does the angle matter?

When you project data onto a line, each point becomes its "shadow" on that line. If the projection direction aligns with the data's natural stretch, shadows spread out widely — high variance. If misaligned, shadows bunch together — low variance, information lost.

Projected variance = λ · cos²(θ)
λ = true variance, θ = angle between projection and true direction
Maximum when θ = 0° (perfectly aligned)
04 · The process

PCA step by step

1
Standardize the data
Apply Z-score normalization so all features have mean=0 and std=1. PCA is sensitive to scale — without this, features with large values dominate the principal components.
2
Compute the covariance matrix
Calculate how every feature varies with every other feature. High covariance between Math & Physics tells us they move together — one component can represent both. The covariance matrix captures all these relationships.
3
Find eigenvectors & eigenvalues
Solve the equation Σ·v = λ·v. Eigenvectors (v) are the directions of the new axes — the principal components. Eigenvalues (λ) tell us how much variance each direction captures. Larger eigenvalue = more important direction.
4
Sort by eigenvalue (largest first)
PC1 has the largest eigenvalue — it captures the most variance. PC2 has the second largest, and so on. The first few PCs typically capture 90–95% of total variance.
5
Keep top k components
Choose how many components to keep based on how much variance you want to preserve. Transform the original data: Z = X · W, where W is the matrix of top k eigenvectors. Each column of Z is a principal component.

06 · The math

Eigenvalues & eigenvectors — demystified

These terms sound intimidating. They're just the mathematical way of finding the best rotation directions. Here's the full calculation for a 2×2 case.

Given covariance matrix C
C = | 4 2 | | 2 3 |

Step 1 — Characteristic equation

Eigenvalues λ satisfy: det(C − λI) = 0

det | 4-λ 2 | = 0 | 2 3-λ | (4-λ)(3-λ) - (2)(2) = 0 12 - 7λ + λ² - 4 = 0 λ² - 7λ + 8 = 0 Solving: λ₁ = 1, λ₂ = 6

Step 2 — Find eigenvectors

For each eigenvalue, solve (C − λI)·v = 0

For λ₂ = 6 (PC1)
| 4-6 2 | |x| = |0| | 2 3-6 | |y| |0| |-2 2| |x| = |0| | 2 -3| |y| |0| From row 1: -2x + 2y = 0 → y = x v₂ = (1/√2)|1| ≈ |0.71| |1| |0.71|
For λ₁ = 1 (PC2)
| 4-1 2 | |x| = |0| | 2 3-1 | |y| |0| | 3 2| |x| = |0| | 2 2| |y| |0| From row 2: 2x + 2y = 0 → y = -x v₁ = (1/√2)| 1| ≈ | 0.71| |-1| |-0.71|
Interpretation
λ₂ = 6 (large)PC1 — captures most variance → keep this
λ₁ = 1 (small)PC2 — captures less variance → may discard
Eigenvector of PC1[0.71, 0.71] — 45° diagonal direction
Total variance explained by PC16/(6+1) = 85.7%
pythonsklearn PCA
from sklearn.decomposition import PCA
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler

# Step 1: Standardize
scaler = StandardScaler()
X_scaled = scaler.fit_transform(X)

# Step 2: Apply PCA — keep 2 components
pca = PCA(n_components=2)
X_pca = pca.fit_transform(X_scaled)

# Check how much variance each component explains
print(pca.explained_variance_ratio_)
# e.g. [0.857, 0.143] — PC1 explains 85.7%
07 · Practical guidance

When to use PCA — and when not to

Use PCA when
Many correlated features✓
High-dimensional data✓
Need 2D/3D visualization✓
Speed matters✓
Image compression✓
Avoid PCA when
Need feature interpretability✗
Features are already uncorrelated✗
Must keep original meaning✗
Important: After PCA, features lose their original meaning. PC1 is not "CGPA." It's something like 0.4·CGPA + 0.3·Internals + 0.2·Study hours... You gain compression but lose explainability. Choose accordingly.
Real-world applications of PCA
Image compressionReduce 10,000 pixels to 100 components
Face recognitionEigenfaces — PCA on face image matrices
Noise removalLow-variance components often capture noise
GenomicsReduce thousands of gene expression features
VisualizationReduce any dataset to 2D for scatter plots
08 · Check your understanding

Quick quiz

1. In PCA, what does the eigenvalue associated with a principal component tell you?
A. The original feature names in the component
B. How much variance that component captures ✓
C. The number of data points in the component
D. Whether to keep or discard the component
The eigenvalue tells you the amount of variance captured by that principal component. Larger eigenvalue = more important direction. PC1 always has the largest eigenvalue — it captures the most information. ✓
2. After applying PCA, the first two components explain 92% of total variance. You discard the remaining components. What happens?
A. All information is lost
B. The model will definitely overfit
C. You lose 8% of variance — usually acceptable ✓
D. The original features are restored
Discarding components that explain only 8% total variance is usually a good trade-off — you get a much simpler model with minimal information loss. In practice, 90–95% variance retention is the standard target. ✓
3. Why must you standardize data BEFORE applying PCA?
A. To remove duplicate rows
B. To convert categorical features
C. Because PCA is scale-sensitive — large-scale features would dominate ✓
D. To increase the number of principal components
PCA finds directions of maximum variance. If Salary is in the range 0–10,00,000 and CGPA is 0–10, Salary's variance dominates purely due to scale — not importance. Standardizing (mean=0, std=1) puts everyone on equal footing. ✓
